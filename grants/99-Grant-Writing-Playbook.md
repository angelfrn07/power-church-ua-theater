# Grant-Getting 101 — Power Church Building Committee Playbook

> The four letters in this folder are starting drafts. *This document* is the playbook that turns those drafts into approved grants. Read it once end-to-end. Reference it before every application.

---

## Part 1 — The Mindset Shift

### Grants are not free money. They are funded relationships.

Every grant Pastor Manny will ever receive comes from a real person at a real foundation who has decided that funding the project will produce real outcomes they can defend at *their* board meeting. The application is the artifact, but the trust-building is the work. First-time applicants who understand this win at materially higher rates than first-time applicants who treat grants as paperwork.

Three operating truths:

1. **Most first applications get declined.** Industry-wide, first-time applicants to a new funder are declined far more often than they are funded — even when the project is excellent. That is not failure. That is the funder learning who you are. Treat the *first* application as the deposit; the *second* application is when you start withdrawing.

2. **The grants officer is your ally, not your gatekeeper.** Raul Rodarte-Suto at Amarillo Area Foundation and Tracy Selle at Bivins want to fund worthy projects. Their job is not to keep money away from East Amarillo families — their job is to make sure the money goes to the strongest applications. A pre-submission call is the single highest-leverage hour in the entire process.

3. **Sustained giving comes from sustained reporting.** A funder who gives $20,000 once is a transaction. A funder who has watched Power Church report results, host a site visit, and execute on the program is a multi-year partner who funds two, three, five times. The application is the start of a relationship, not the end of one.

### The numbers Pastor Manny needs in his head before he writes anything

Funders want to see specific numbers. If the narrative says "many families," they will translate that into "this applicant doesn't know their own program." If it says "approximately 200 families per week," they translate it as "this applicant runs their program."

Memorize, or write on a post-it next to the laptop:

- **Power Church founded:** 2012
- **Power Church average attendance:** 1,700–2,000
- **Sunday food pantry started:** 2017
- **Sunday food pantry partner:** High Plains Food Bank
- **Current pantry capacity:** ~50 families/week (early 2017 baseline) — 200 families per distribution event in recent years
- **Target pantry capacity at the new building:** 150–200 families/week sustained
- **Barrio Bash attendance, 2017:** ~3,000
- **ABCD 501(c)(3) founded:** 2015 (IRS-recognized 2019)
- **ABCD EIN:** 47-4527312
- **The new building:** 8275 W Amarillo Boulevard, 83,422 sq ft, on 10 acres
- **Acquisition price:** $3.8M
- **Bank:** Amarillo National Bank
- **Renovation estimate:** ~$2M (subject to final GC bid)
- **Targeted post-renovation use:** sanctuary + food pantry + daycare + youth after-school + classrooms + lobby café + media studio + community-event venue

These numbers are the bones of every grant application Pastor Manny will write for the next two years.

## Part 2 — The 60-Day Timeline Before Any Grant Deadline

For grants Pastor Manny is filing this month (AAF Catalyst, Bivins), the timeline below is compressed because we're starting from a standing start. **For all future grants, work backwards from the deadline using this template:**

### 60 days before deadline

- Identify the grant program. Read the funder's website, their last three years of annual reports, and (if available) their list of past grantees. Match Power Church / ABCD's project to one or two past grantees that look similar — that is your fit-test signal.
- Identify the right entity to apply (Power Church or ABCD) using the routing logic in the grant-finder skill.
- Pull the application packet. Note every required document.

### 45 days before deadline

- Email or call the grants officer. Request a 15–20 minute pre-submission call. Use the email template in `01-AAF-Catalyst-Pre-Submission-Email.md` as a model.
- Begin gathering supporting documents: 501(c)(3) letter, latest 990, board roster, financial statements.

### 30 days before deadline

- Hold the pre-submission call. Listen more than you talk. Ask the three questions in Part 3 below.
- Draft the application narrative.
- Identify and request letters of support from partner organizations. Letters typically take partners 7–14 days to produce, sometimes longer. Don't wait.

### 14 days before deadline

- Send the draft to one trusted reader (a board member, a fellow pastor, a friend in the nonprofit world). Ask them: "Would you fund this with your own money?" If the answer is "I'd want more detail on X," fix X.
- Confirm all supporting documents are in hand.
- Begin filling out the funder's portal — *not* on the deadline day. Most portals have quirks (character limits that don't match what they say, file-size limits, dropdown menus that lock fields). Find out a week early.

### 7 days before deadline

- Final read-through. Look specifically for:
  - Numbers that match across narrative + budget + supporting docs
  - Names spelled correctly (every funder has been burned by an application that mis-spelled their founder's name)
  - The funder's exact priorities echoed in the narrative
- Submit. Do not wait until the deadline day.

### Deadline day

- Confirm submission. Most portals send a confirmation email — save it. If you don't get one, call the grants office.
- Send a brief thank-you to the grants officer who took the pre-submission call.

### After submission

- Add the funder's decision date to your calendar. Most foundations announce within 60–90 days.
- Do *not* email the grants officer chasing the decision. Wait.

## Part 3 — The Pre-Submission Consultation Playbook

This is the highest-leverage 20 minutes in the entire process. Pastor Manny should take it seriously and prepare for it like he prepares for a Sunday sermon.

### Before the call

Have ready, on paper:

1. A **one-paragraph project summary** — what is being requested, what it funds, what outcomes result. The same paragraph that will lead the application narrative.
2. **The dollar amount** Power Church / ABCD plans to request.
3. **The deadline** — confirm you both know what cycle this is for.
4. **The two or three past grantees** of this funder who look most similar to Power Church / ABCD's project. ("I noticed you funded XYZ last year for a similar program — that informed our framing.")
5. **A list of 3–5 questions** specific to your application.

### What Pastor Manny actually says on the call

The call should be ~70% Pastor Manny listening, ~30% Pastor Manny talking. Open like this:

> "Thank you for taking the call. Before I waste your time on details that don't fit, I want to confirm that what we're planning is the right kind of project for the [Catalyst Cycle / Community Grant]. May I describe the project in two minutes, and then ask you a few questions?"

That sentence does three things:
- Signals respect for the grants officer's time
- Frames Pastor Manny as someone who knows applications get declined, not someone who feels entitled to be funded
- Earns permission to talk

### The three questions to always ask

1. **"Is this the right fit for this funder right now?"** Funders sometimes shift priorities year-to-year; a program that was a fit in 2024 may not be in 2026. Ask directly.

2. **"Of the elements I described, which two or three do you think we should lead with in the application?"** This question turns the grants officer into your co-author. They will tell you the priority signals their board responds to — information that is nowhere on the funder's website.

3. **"What's the one mistake first-time applicants make with this program that we should make sure to avoid?"** This question will save Pastor Manny from twenty hours of needless work. The grants officer has seen 200 applications. They know exactly where they fail.

### After the call

Send a thank-you email within 24 hours. Two paragraphs maximum. Include:
- Specific gratitude for one thing they said that was useful
- A brief note that you will incorporate their feedback into the application
- One line confirming the submission deadline

This email also creates a paper trail proving the consultation happened — useful if the application later comes down to a tiebreaker.

## Part 4 — The Application Narrative Anatomy

Most grant applications follow a similar shape, even when funders use different wording for their fields. The seven-part structure below maps cleanly to AAF, Bivins, Walmart Spark Good, and most foundation forms.

### 1. Project Summary (one paragraph, 150–200 words)

Answers four questions in order:
- What is the project?
- Who does it serve?
- What does the grant specifically fund?
- What outcome does the funded portion produce?

Look at the project summaries in `03-AAF-Catalyst-Application-Narrative.md` and `04-Bivins-Foundation-Application-Narrative.md` for examples.

### 2. The Need (1–2 paragraphs)

What problem are you solving? Be specific to your community. Don't say "Amarillo has food insecurity." Say "Power Church's weekly food pantry has been turning families away because the SE 10th Avenue building's kitchen and parking can't support the volume our community needs."

The need section should make the funder feel the gap before they read about your solution.

### 3. The Opportunity (1 paragraph)

What changed? Why now? In Power Church's case: "The 8275 W Amarillo Boulevard facility was built with a commercial-grade kitchen footprint. We have the chance to convert that infrastructure into a community food-pantry production space — but only if we move now, while it's available and within our financial reach."

The opportunity section converts the problem into a moment.

### 4. What the Grant Funds (line-item budget + descriptive paragraph)

Funders want to know specifically how their dollars will be spent. A budget that lists "supplies — $5,000" is weaker than a budget that lists "Food pantry intake forms, dignity packets, transportation reimbursement for volunteer drivers — $5,000." Specificity builds trust.

Use the budget tables in the existing application drafts as templates.

### 5. Outcomes Expected

How will you know it worked? Funders want measurable, time-bound outcomes:

- "Expand weekly distribution capacity from ~50 to ~150–200 families per week within 12 months."
- "Serve approximately 40 East Amarillo children per afternoon, five days per week, in the first program year."
- "Provide case management or warm referrals for approximately 100 East Amarillo families per year."

If a funder asks for outcomes and the application says "many people will be helped," the funder knows the applicant doesn't know how to run a program. Specific is professional.

### 6. Sustainability (the question funders care about second-most)

Funders are afraid of giving you money for a program that disappears the day their grant runs out. Show them how the program continues:

- Other grants in the pipeline (name them)
- Earned revenue (rent, fees, partnerships)
- Donor pledges (general number is fine)
- The lowest-cost expression — what it would look like if you got NO additional funding

This last one matters. Be honest. "If we received no further funding, the after-school program would scale back to 15 children per afternoon supported by Power Church's general operating budget" is more credible than "we will definitely get another grant."

### 7. Why Us / Why Now (1–2 paragraphs)

Who is this applicant and what makes them able to deliver? List proof of execution:

- Track record (Barrio Bash since 2017, food pantry since 2017, Barrio Community Watch with APD since 2017, Faith Community Summit 2022, Healing Prayer Walk 2026)
- Partnerships (High Plains Food Bank, APD, City of Amarillo, Potter County)
- Governance (board, 501(c)(3) status, financial discipline)
- Local presence (Pastor Manny's role in East Amarillo)

This is the section where Pastor Manny's nine-year track record stops being humility and starts being underwriting.

## Part 5 — Budgets That Get Funded

The budget is where the most newbie applications get downgraded. Learn this.

### Be specific to the line item

Strong budget lines: "Commercial walk-in cooler and freezer (refurbished) — $18,000"
Weak budget lines: "Equipment — $18,000"

### Match the budget to the narrative

If the narrative says "we will hire two part-time case managers," the budget should have a line for case-manager salaries. If the budget has a line not mentioned in the narrative, the application looks careless.

### Cap admin / overhead

Most funders allow 5–10% for administrative overhead. Cap yours at 5% to signal that you're efficient. If you ask for 15% overhead, the funder mentally penalizes the rest of the budget.

### Show match and in-kind

Funders love to see that they're not the only one investing. Show:
- **Cash match:** Power Church capital reserves, ABCD reserves, capital-campaign pledges
- **In-kind match:** Volunteer hours (estimate at $30/hour), donated facility space (estimate based on local commercial rent), donated professional services (legal, accounting)

A grant that shows "$20,000 from the Foundation, $15,000 in church reserves, $30,000 in-kind from volunteers and donated space = $65,000 total project investment" is dramatically stronger than a grant that shows only the $20,000 ask.

### Keep budget total = sum of line items

This sounds obvious. Newbies fail it constantly. If your line items add to $19,950 and your "Total" line says $20,000, the grants officer will assume you don't run your books carefully. Re-add. Always.

## Part 6 — Letters of Support

A letter of support is a partner organization saying "we have agreed to work with this applicant on this project." For Power Church / ABCD, the partners that matter most are:

- **High Plains Food Bank** — Tony Carbajal, CEO. He runs the partnership Power Church already has. Letter should confirm the partnership, the volume capacity expectations, and the fit with HPFB's mission.
- **Amarillo Police Department** — the Barrio Community Watch coordinator and (ideally) the chief or assistant chief. Letter should confirm the 9-year community-policing partnership and support for community-safety programming at the new building.
- **Amarillo National Bank** — letter of intent or executed term sheet for the acquisition financing. This is not technically a "letter of support" but it functions as one in capital-grant applications because it proves the building purchase will actually close.
- **Amarillo Independent School District** — if any after-school programming partnership is planned, AISD's District Partnerships office (806-326-1013) is the right place to ask.

### How to ask for a letter of support

Email or call the partner. Subject line: "Letter of support request — [Project Name]."

In the email:
1. Briefly describe the grant and the project.
2. State the deadline (give them at least 14 days).
3. **Offer to draft the letter for them.** Most partner organizations are too busy to write a letter from scratch. If you draft it on Power Church / ABCD letterhead and email a Word doc, the partner usually edits, signs, and returns it.

### What a strong letter of support says

- States the partnership exists (date it started, what it has produced)
- Confirms the partner endorses the project
- Names a specific partner contribution (volunteer hours, food, equipment, space, expertise)
- Closes with the partner's name, title, and contact info on their letterhead

### What to do AFTER you receive the letter

Send a thank-you. Not because the partner expects it — because the partner is now part of the project's network and you want them invested next time too.

## Part 7 — Submission Day, the Thank-You, and the Progress Report

### Submission day

- Submit *at least* 24 hours before the deadline. Portals crash the last hour of every grant cycle.
- Print or save a PDF of the submitted application. Funders sometimes lose submissions.
- Save the confirmation email.

### The thank-you note (after submission, before the decision)

This is optional. Some funders explicitly ask you not to. Read the funder's instructions.

If thank-you notes are welcome, send a one-paragraph email or hand-written note within 48 hours of submission. Address it to the grants officer who took the pre-submission call. Mention one specific way their guidance shaped the application.

### The decision

If FUNDED:
- Send a thank-you the day you hear. Not the next day. The day.
- Plan a site visit invitation: "We'd love to host you at our food pantry distribution one Sunday morning if that's a fit for your schedule."
- Begin reporting against the milestones in the application immediately. Do not wait for the funder to ask.

If DECLINED:
- Send a thank-you anyway. Be gracious.
- Email the grants officer one week later: "We received the news. Thank you again. If you have time, would you be willing to share what made the application not the right fit, so we can strengthen our next submission?" Most grants officers will take this call. The information they share is gold.
- File the application. Re-apply next cycle with everything they said incorporated. Funders fund persistent applicants.

### The progress report (if funded)

Most foundations require a 6-month or 12-month report. Treat this as the most important application you'll write all year — because it determines whether the next grant gets funded.

A strong progress report:
- Reports actuals against the outcomes you promised
- Shares one specific story (anonymized) of a family or person served
- Includes 2–4 photos
- Is honest about what worked and what didn't
- Asks for a meeting to discuss the next phase

The progress report is also where Pastor Manny earns the right to ask for a larger grant next cycle.

## Part 8 — Newbie Traps to Avoid

In rough order of how often they kill applications:

1. **Generic narrative.** Copy-pasting the same paragraph across funders. Funders can tell. Each application should reference *that funder's* specific priorities and *that funder's* specific past grantees.

2. **Asking for too much.** First-time applicants who ask for $250K from a foundation that gives a median grant of $25K signal they didn't read the funder's pages. Ask within range; build relationship; ask larger next time.

3. **Asking for too little.** Inversely: don't underprice yourself. If a funder gives $5K–$50K and your project genuinely needs $40K, ask for $40K. Don't reflexively ask for $10K because it feels safer.

4. **Sloppy math in the budget.** Total ≠ sum of line items. Hourly rates that don't multiply correctly. Inconsistencies between narrative and budget. Funders hate this.

5. **Skipping the pre-submission call.** This is by far the highest-leverage hour available. Skipping it is leaving 30% of your win probability on the table.

6. **Last-minute submission.** Portals crash. Files corrupt. Internet drops. Submit a day early.

7. **Not asking partner organizations for letters of support early enough.** Partners need 14+ days. Applications without letters of support look thin even when the project is excellent.

8. **Failing to acknowledge faith status when relevant.** Some funders explicitly fund faith-based orgs; some explicitly don't; many are silent. Read the funder's eligibility carefully. When silent, ASK before assuming.

9. **Promising specific dollar match without being able to document it.** If you say "Power Church will match $25K from operating reserves," that match must be in writing in church board minutes — funders sometimes ask. Don't promise what you can't document.

10. **Disappearing after a decline.** Most first-time applicants who get declined never apply again. Funders fund persistent applicants. Re-apply next cycle.

11. **Disappearing after an award.** This kills future fundraising more than any other mistake. Report on time. Send photos. Invite for a site visit. The funder paid for a relationship; honor it.

12. **Treating the grants officer like a customer service rep.** They are a peer professional doing their job. Respect their time, follow their instructions, never argue if the application is declined.

## Part 9 — Faith-Based Considerations (Specific to Power Church / ABCD)

### The Establishment Clause and grants

Federal funds and many state and foundation programs cannot be used for inherently religious activities (worship services, sectarian instruction, religious counseling). They CAN fund community programs operated by religious organizations as long as the funded activities are themselves secular. This is why the routing strategy matters:

- **Power Church** is the right applicant for: Christian-aligned foundations (Bivins historically), capital projects where the worship use is peripheral to community use, denominational grants, faith-friendly community grants (Walmart Spark Good is faith-friendly).
- **Amarillo Barrio Community Development (ABCD)** is the right applicant for: federal programs (USDA Community Facilities, HUD Section 4, DOJ Project Safe Neighborhoods), foundations that exclude houses of worship, faith-silent funders where neutrality is safer.

### When in doubt, ask

The cleanest move when a funder's faith-eligibility is unclear is to ask. "Does the Foundation fund 501(c)(3) churches when the funded activities are community programs?" — this question takes one minute on a phone call and removes a major risk.

### How to talk about faith in applications

In ABCD applications: don't lead with faith. Lead with mission ("alleviate poverty in East Amarillo"). Reference partnerships with faith organizations (Power Church) the same way you'd reference a partnership with a civic organization (APD).

In Power Church applications to faith-friendly funders (Bivins, Christian foundations): faith framing is welcomed. You can say "Power Church's food-pantry ministry is the most direct expression we know of one of the simplest commands in the Christian faith — feed the hungry."

In Power Church applications to faith-silent secular funders: stay close to mission language. "Power Church operates a weekly food pantry in partnership with the High Plains Food Bank serving 50–200 families per week" is something a secular funder fully understands without needing the theological framing.

### The donor sensitivity issue

Some Power Church members may be sensitive to "the church accepting government money." Pastor Manny knows his congregation better than any consultant; this is a pastoral judgment. Some considerations:

- **ABCD-routed grants resolve almost all of this concern.** Members donate to Power Church; ABCD is a separate 501(c)(3) doing community-development work; ABCD applies for community grants from government and secular sources. Members typically have no objection to ABCD pursuing those funds because that's exactly what ABCD was founded to do.
- **Foundation grants from Christian-aligned funders (Bivins) are usually uncontroversial** — Mary E. Bivins was a Christian woman who built a foundation specifically to fund Christian-aligned causes.
- **Federal capital grants like USDA Community Facilities** can be the most sensitive in some congregations. If Pastor Manny is unsure, he can route the federal apps through ABCD specifically and keep Power Church's name out of those applications.

## Part 10 — Amarillo-Specific Tactics

Amarillo is small enough that relationships travel. Use it.

### Who knows who

The community of grant officers and nonprofit leaders in Amarillo is small. Raul Rodarte-Suto knows Tracy Selle. Both of them know the leadership at the High Plains Food Bank, the United Way of Amarillo & Canyon, Catholic Charities of the Texas Panhandle, Family Support Services, and Hope and Healing Place. A strong impression with one grants officer becomes a strong reputation across the entire local funding network within months.

### Walking-around capital

Every Amarillo grants officer has *discretionary* funds — small, fast, no-application grants they can give to a project they believe in. The way to access these isn't to ask for them. It's to build the relationship through a regular grant cycle, then mention casually "we have a small gap in [X] — do you know anyone who funds that quickly?" Sometimes the answer is "yes, send me an email, I'll see what I can do."

This is *only* available to applicants who have built actual trust. Don't try this on a first email.

### Local bank corporate giving

Amarillo National Bank (Power Church's banking relationship), FirstBank Southwest, Happy State Bank, and Education Credit Union all have community-giving programs. They are typically smaller ($500–$5,000), faster (no formal cycle for some), and incredibly relationship-driven. Pastor Manny should ask his banker at ANB *during* the loan-package conversations whether ANB has a community-giving program he can apply to. Banks love to fund community projects they're also financing — it doubles their CRA credit.

### Don't underestimate the Texas Panhandle Tech Association, Service Clubs, and Civic Groups

Rotary, Lions Club, Kiwanis, Knights of Columbus, Amarillo Garden Club — smaller but real. A $1,000 grant from Rotary plus a Rotary letter of support is sometimes worth more than a $10,000 grant alone, because the Rotary network introduces Power Church to dozens of business owners who become individual donors.

### The Amarillo Globe-News test

Before submitting any application, ask: "Would Power Church / ABCD be comfortable having this story written up in the Amarillo Globe-News?" If yes, good. If no, the application has framing that needs to be cleaned up. Funders sometimes share grantee stories with local media; the framing in the application becomes the framing in the press.

## Part 11 — Tools, Templates, and Resources

### Master file checklist (gather these once, reuse forever)

Power Church + ABCD should each maintain a folder (digital, organized) containing:

- Most recent IRS 501(c)(3) determination letter
- Latest IRS Form 990 (or 990-N if applicable)
- Latest audited or compiled financial statement
- Latest two years of board-approved annual budgets
- Current board of directors roster with affiliations and bios
- Mission statement (the one-sentence version and the one-paragraph version)
- 2–3 program-impact stories (anonymized, ~300 words each)
- 10–20 recent program photos (high resolution, with people's faces *only* if you have written photo releases)
- Floor plans, site photos, exterior photos of all church property
- Two professional bios for Pastor Manny (one paragraph and one full page)
- Letter-of-support template that partners can edit and sign

Every grant application pulls from this folder. Maintaining it well saves 8–15 hours per application.

### AI usage tips for grant drafting

Pastor Manny does not need to become a grant writer overnight. Use AI like he uses a sermon-prep assistant:

- **Use AI to first-draft.** Hand it the project summary, the funder's priorities, and the budget. Let it produce a 1,000-word draft.
- **Edit ruthlessly.** First drafts from AI are always too long, too generic, and too lifeless. Cut hard. Replace abstract language with specifics from the numbers list in Part 1.
- **Have AI read the draft like a grants officer.** "Read this as if you are a skeptical grants officer at Mary E. Bivins Foundation. What questions do you have? Where would you mark it down?" The answer will tell Pastor Manny what to fix.
- **Don't let AI invent numbers, dates, partners, or quotes.** Anything specific must come from real records. AI is a writing assistant, not a fact source.

### When to hire a grant writer

After Power Church wins its first 2–3 grants, consider hiring a part-time grant writer ($20–$50 per hour locally, or $400–$1,500 per application on a contract basis). They earn their fee on a single mid-sized grant. Look for grant writers experienced with religious nonprofits AND community-development organizations — most specialize in one or the other; you want both.

The Amarillo Area Foundation can sometimes recommend local grant writers. Just ask Raul.
